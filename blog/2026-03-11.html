Brother Mine,

Have you ever watched those vlogbrothers videos on YouTube? Public video correspondence between John and Hank Green, famous authors and YouTube educators? They are both very brilliant men, and kind and funny and interesting. John has written many best-selling YA novels; Hank is a science communcator.

I like how they talk and relate to each other, but they are also talking publicly, to the world. There is something valuable about personal, private correspondence, but there is also something differently valuable about public correspondence. I think it would be nice to have one of those with you.

I’d be talking to you about things we both find interesting. But they are also open letters, with a broader secondary audience, which is really not so secondary.

When I write public blog posts, it doesn’t feel right to me. It feels like I’m being both narcissistic and pathetic. I have something I want to say to the world!! Look at me! But probably no one is reading. It doesn’t matter that I don’t have a wide audience, but there has always been something weird and sad about people who write blogs to an anonymous audience. Who are they to think anyone would want to hear what they have to say? Yet at the same time, I’ve always wanted to do it.

Addressing my posts to you would feel so much more natual. I’d still want to write you private texts, emails, chats, phone calls, etc. I still want to write you typed letters and mail them by international post. But an open correspondence–dispatches to each other from opposite sides of the world–would give a life to my public utterances that they probably wouldn’t otherwise have.

I know I want to write more. A lot more. Reading and writing animate my soul. But writing to no audience or just to myself hasn’t taken me very far as a writer. Writers, for whatever reason, need an audience. I have an ambivalent relationship with that fact. One the one hand, I’m sort of like J. D. Salinger, who was suspicious of any and all kinds of inauthentic art, which he seems to have defined as something made to impress someone else. He took that so far as to stop writing for an audience at all, and only write private novels for himself, with the idea that either no one would ever read them, or that they would be published after his death. I’m not sure how that worked out for him.

But having an audience doesn’t have to be phony. Having an audience is about sharing your thoughts, ideas, loves, and experiences with your fellow human beings. There is nothing wrong with that. In fact, it’s essential. I don’t think many people out there would or should care much about what I have to say. But I think you do. And I care about what you have to write and compose and film. I would love to use that as inspiration.

We could start by using our own personal blogs and addressing certain posts to each other. We don’t have to respond to everything in them. We can still make comments. But some of my blogs would start with “Brother mine,” and end with “Vale.” (The way Pliny the Younger used to sign off in his published letters. We could decide later if we want a dedicated blog for the correspondence and how that would work, technically. But the way it is now, we’d be notified via RSS of each other’s posts.

What do you think?
